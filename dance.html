Understanding Bharatanatyam

Bharatanatyam is one of the major forms of Indian classical dance originating in Tamil Nadu. Tracing its lineage over 2,000 years, it is rooted in the Natya Shastra and was historically performed as a form of temple worship.

The art form integrates three core elements: Nritta (pure rhythmic movement), Nritya (expressive dance conveying emotions), and Natya (dramatic narrative).

Dancers undergo years of training in adavus (footwork patterns), mudras (hand gestures), and abhinaya (facial expressions) to master this sacred art form.

Understanding Kathak

Kathak is one of the eight major forms of Indian classical dance. Originating from the storytelling bards of North India, Kathak is characterized by lightning-fast footwork, graceful spins (chakkar), and vivid storytelling through movement.

It evolved under both Hindu and Muslim patronage, developing distinct styles across three main gharanas: Lucknow (grace and emotion), Jaipur (powerful technique), and Benares (devotional fervour).

Kathak uniquely blends Hindu religious narratives with Mughal court elegance, making it a bridge between two great artistic traditions of India.

Understanding Kuchipudi

Kuchipudi is a classical dance form that originated in Kuchipudi village of Krishna district, Andhra Pradesh. Traditionally performed by Brahmin men in traveling troupes, it blends dance and drama to portray stories from Hindu epics.

Its most celebrated element is the Tarangam — in which the dancer performs intricate movements while balancing a water pot on the head and dancing on the narrow rim of a brass plate.

Kuchipudi is known for its graceful hand gestures, expressive eye movements, rhythmic footwork, and theatrical elements that transform a stage into a divine narrative.
