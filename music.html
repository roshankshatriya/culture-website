Three Sacred Streams

Each tradition a universe of its own — rooted in the same soil, yet flowering differently.

Carnatic Music

The devotional classical music tradition of South India with roots in ancient Hindu scriptures. Built around 72 Melakarta ragas, it celebrates raga and tala with compositions called Kritis.

Hindustani Music

North India's classical tradition shaped by Persian and Islamic influences. Known for its improvisational nature through alap, jor, and jhala, using khayal, dhrupad, and thumri forms.

South Indian Classical

The World of Carnatic Music

Carnatic music is one of the two main sub-genres of Indian classical music, developed mostly in the southern states. It is primarily devotional in nature, with the majority of compositions written in praise of Hindu deities.

The structure of Carnatic music is built on Ragas (melodic scales) and Talas (rhythmic cycles). The 72 Melakarta raga system, classified by Venkatamakhin in the 17th century, forms the foundation of the tradition.

Key composers like Thyagaraja, Muthuswami Dikshitar, and Syama Sastri — collectively called the Trinity of Carnatic Music — created thousands of compositions that remain central to the repertoire today.

North Indian Classical

The Universe of Hindustani Music

Hindustani classical music, the music of North India, evolved from Vedic traditions and was deeply influenced by Persian and Mughal court culture from the 13th century onward. This fusion gave birth to a uniquely expressive art form.

At the core of Hindustani music are Ragas — melodic frameworks with specific ascending and descending scale patterns, characteristic phrases, and emotional moods. Each raga is associated with a particular time of day or season.

Major forms include Dhrupad (the oldest), Khayal (most widely practiced today), Thumri (semi-classical and romantic), and Ghazal (poetic and lyrical).

Instruments

Sacred Instruments

The divine voices of India — instruments that have carried ragas and talas across centuries.

Veena

The mother of all string instruments, associated with Goddess Saraswati. Central to Carnatic music with its rich, resonant tonal quality.

Mridangam

The primary percussion instrument in Carnatic music, with roots going back over 2,000 years to ancient temple traditions.

Bansuri

The bamboo flute, eternally associated with Lord Krishna. Its haunting, breathy tone carries both earthly longing and spiritual transcendence.

Sitar

The iconic plucked string instrument of Hindustani music, with 20 strings and intricate sympathetic resonators that produce a shimmering sound.

Tabla

A pair of hand drums central to Hindustani classical music. Its sophisticated bols (syllables) create complex rhythmic tapestries that complement the melody.

Tanpura

The foundational drone instrument providing the tonal basis for both Carnatic and Hindustani performances — the sonic canvas on which ragas are painted.

Violin

Adopted into Carnatic music in the 18th century, the violin has become an inseparable part of South Indian classical concerts, played in a unique seated posture.

Sarod

A fretless string instrument of Hindustani music descended from the Afghan rabab, known for its deep, weighty tone and intricate note-bending capabilities.

Ghungroo

The ankle bells worn by classical dancers — tied in strings of 50–200 bells, their rhythmic chiming adds a percussive dimension to dance performances.
